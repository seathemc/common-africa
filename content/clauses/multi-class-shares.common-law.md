# Multi-class Share Structure — Common-law Drafting

> **Status:** Draft v0.1 — for community review. Not legal advice. Adapt with qualified counsel before use.

## Purpose

To enable a labelled startup, incorporated under the company law of a common-law jurisdiction (e.g. Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, South Africa), to issue more than one class of shares with differentiated voting, dividend, and liquidation rights — without resorting to an offshore holding structure.

## Model articles (insert into the company's articles of association)

### 1. Authorised classes

The Company is authorised to issue ordinary shares, preferred shares, and non-voting shares. The board may, by resolution and within limits set by the shareholders in general meeting, designate sub-classes (e.g. Series Seed, Series A) and fix the rights, restrictions and preferences attaching to each.

### 2. Voting rights

Each ordinary share carries one vote on every matter submitted to a vote of the shareholders. Preferred shares carry the voting rights specified at issuance. Non-voting shares carry no voting rights, save in respect of (a) any variation of the rights attaching to that class, and (b) any resolution to wind up the Company.

### 3. Liquidation preference

On a Liquidation Event, the holders of preferred shares shall be entitled to receive, in priority to ordinary and non-voting shareholders, an amount equal to one times (1×) the original issue price per share, plus any declared but unpaid dividends. Thereafter, the remaining assets shall be distributed pro rata among the holders of ordinary and non-voting shares.

"Liquidation Event" means (i) a liquidation, dissolution or winding-up of the Company; (ii) a sale of all or substantially all of the Company's assets; or (iii) a transaction or series of related transactions in which more than 50% of the Company's voting power changes hands.

### 4. Anti-dilution

If the Company issues new shares at a price per share lower than the price paid by holders of any class of preferred shares, the conversion price of those preferred shares shall be adjusted on a broad-based weighted-average basis.

### 5. Conversion

Each preferred share is convertible at the option of the holder, at any time, into one ordinary share, subject to adjustment under section 4. All preferred shares shall convert automatically into ordinary shares immediately prior to (a) a qualified initial public offering, or (b) the written election of the holders of a majority of the then-outstanding preferred shares.

### 6. Statutory carve-out

Where the prevailing companies legislation enforces a default one-share-one-vote rule, this clause shall be read together with any startup-act provision permitting labelled startups to depart from that rule. In the event of conflict, the startup-act provision shall prevail in respect of the Company.

## Implementation notes

- **Nigeria:** Requires interaction with CAMA s. 124. Until a surgical amendment is enacted, founders typically combine this drafting with shareholder agreements granting weighted voting via proxy. The Common framework recommends a one-line CAMA amendment recognising the right of NSA-labelled startups to issue multi-class shares.
- **Kenya:** Companies Act 2015 permits classes of shares; this drafting works within the existing statutory framework.
- **Ghana:** Companies Act 2019 (Act 992) permits classes; align with KENIA-equivalent labelling once the Startup Act passes.
- **South Africa:** Companies Act 71 of 2008 expressly permits more than one class of shares; the constraint is practice, not statute.
